Part 1

Why the Gender Split Matters More Than Any Other Metric

When people talk about growing an audience, they usually mean "get more followers" or "run more ads." Those are lagging indicators. The actual leading indicator — the thing that determines whether Spotify's algorithm will carry you to millions of new listeners for free — is the quality of engagement signals your existing audience generates.

And here's the uncomfortable truth about a 60% male / 40% female split in R&B: male listeners, on average, are passive consumers. Female listeners, on average, are active curators. That distinction is everything.

The Algorithmic Behavior Gap

Spotify's algorithm does not care how many streams a song has. It cares about engagement signals — specifically: saves, playlist adds, repeat listens, shares, and low skip rates. These signals are what prompt the algorithm to recommend your music to new listeners via Discover Weekly, Radio, and Autoplay.

Research into streaming behavior (including Spotify's own Loud & Clear data) consistently shows that female listeners generate these high-intent engagement signals at meaningfully higher rates than male listeners in the R&B/soul/atmospheric genre space. The reason is behavioral, not biological: women in this genre actively curate "mood playlists" for night drives, emotional processing, getting ready, and intimate moments. They save songs they feel. Men are more likely to stream passively through algorithmic radio without creating the active engagement signals the algorithm needs to amplify a track.

Save Rate
What % of listeners save a track to their library. Industry benchmark: 20%+. This is the single highest-intent positive signal Spotify reads.
↑ Female listeners: Higher save rates in atmospheric/emotional R&B
Playlist Add Rate
A user adding your song to a personal playlist is a "super-like" signal. The algorithm treats this as long-term value declaration.
↑ Female listeners: Heavy playlist curation behavior ("late night," "feelings," "vibe")
Share Behavior
Songs shared via DM, stories, or SMS trigger Spotify's social graph data and expand the reach model beyond your existing follower base.
↑ Female listeners: Higher "send this song" behavior in relationships
Skip Rate
The algorithm killer. Early skips (before 30 sec) tell Spotify "wrong audience." Low skip rates tell the algorithm the song belongs in the feed.
↑ Female listeners who gravitate to a song: Tend toward full listen + repeat
The Core Insight

A 60/40 M/F split means 60% of your current listeners are generating fewer of the active signals the algorithm needs. Shift that to 45/55 or 40/60 — even just 15–20 percentage points — and your save rate, playlist add rate, and share rate all climb simultaneously. The algorithm then interprets your catalog as "high-value music that people return to and curate with." That is what unlocks Radio and Autoplay placement at scale. That is what happened to Brent Faiyaz between 2020 and 2022.

Part 2

How Brent Faiyaz Actually Did It — Chronological Breakdown

This is not a story of Faiyaz "going pop" or softening his brand. It's a story of him systematically expanding the emotional access points in his music and image while keeping his core identity intact. The gender flip happened across three distinct phases over roughly four years.

2016–2019 · Phase 1: Foundation
Sonder + Early Solo Work — Building the Core
Faiyaz starts as part of Sonder (with Dpat and Atu), building an early underground following in the lo-fi/alternative R&B space. The audience at this stage is majority male — music nerds, producers, people who found him through SoundCloud and Sonder's Bandcamp. The sonic identity is established (dark, atmospheric, introspective) but the emotional range is narrow. Songs are about him and his feelings from his perspective — not yet written in ways that allow the listener to project their own emotional experience onto them easily.
Audience est. 70% M / 30% F · Niche underground
February 2020 · Phase 2: The Breakout
"Fuck the World" EP — Platinum, Billboard 200 #20
This EP is the critical inflection point — but NOT because it captured a female audience. It actually cemented the male core first. The themes — paranoia about fame, distrust of relationships, isolation, "I'm choosing myself" — are deeply relatable to men navigating shallow social environments. The aesthetic is dark, closed-off, intentionally unromantic. Songs like "Clouded" and "Dead Man Walking" are confessional but not vulnerable in the way that signals emotional availability.

What this phase accomplished: It built a large, loyal male base. That base gave him streaming numbers and algorithmic proof. But it also created a ceiling — his audience was passionate but not growing into new demographics. The female side of the split was growing slowly, but mostly women who got him through male partners or music-nerd circles.
~2.04B total streams from this era · Audience still M-dominant
2020–2021 · Phase 2.5: The Bridge Singles
"Gravity," "Dead Man Walking (Remix)," "Wasting Time" — The Pivot
This is the hinge. Three releases in 18 months that functioned as a bridge between his male-coded core and the female audience he needed to reach. Each one introduced a new emotional dimension without abandoning the sonic identity:

"Gravity" (feat. DJ Dahi, 2020): Still dark, but introduced a sense of incompleteness — the feeling of being drawn to someone you can't fully have. This "unfulfilled pull" emotion is highly resonant with women navigating ambiguous relationships. The production is woozy and immersive.

"Wasting Time" (feat. Drake, July 2021): This is the single most important release in the gender flip. Instead of the usual detached/toxic/withholding perspective, Faiyaz is asking for something — "give me your undivided attention." He's showing desire, not just receiving it. He's showing want, not just distance. Combined with The Neptunes' early-2000s production (instantly nostalgic for women in their 20s-30s), this gave women a Faiyaz song they could feel being wanted in — not just observing his pain. TikTok exploded with GRWM (Get Ready With Me) videos, night drive content, and "sending this to my person" posts. The algorithmic save and share signals from this single were categorically different from everything prior.
Wasting Time: Became Faiyaz's highest-charting single pre-Wasteland
July 8, 2022 · Phase 3: Full Flip
"Wasteland" Album — Billboard 200 #2 · Majority Female Audience
This album is the full expression of what "Wasting Time" had unlocked. The production is maximalist — Neptunes, Tyler the Creator, Alicia Keys. The narrative is cinematic, not confessional. But crucially: the emotional themes are no longer just his pain — they're about the space between two people. Longing, tension, the aftermath of intimacy, desire with consequences. These are emotions that resonate across gender lines but historically index higher with female listeners in the R&B space.

The image shift was equally deliberate: Throughout the Wasteland era, Faiyaz leaned into his physical appearance purposefully — editorial shoots, cinematic visual rollouts, the deliberate cultivation of "the man women talk about." He had always been private about his personal life, but now that privacy became mystique rather than just distance. He let the music create emotional availability while maintaining personal inscrutability — this combination (emotionally available in art, personally guarded in life) is the exact formula for female superfan creation.

On social content: He didn't suddenly start doing dance trends or thirst-trap content. He shared fragments — studio sessions, intentional vulnerability, fashion moments, the occasional unfiltered candid. The persona expanded without being performed. Women started sharing his content because it felt authentic, not curated specifically for them.
Wasteland: ~2.70B streams · Billboard #2 · Audience shifts to majority female
2023–2025 · Phase 4: Sovereign
ISO Supremacy JV + Roc Nation — The Leverage Play
With a majority-female audience generating consistent high-intent algorithmic signals, Faiyaz crossed 25M monthly listeners and became undeniable to infrastructure players. He didn't sign away his catalog — he built a joint venture with UnitedMasters for $50M while retaining 100% creative control and ownership. Then he added Roc Nation management in 2026. He didn't beg for this. He created the data that made them come to him.

The gender flip is what made this possible. A majority-female R&B audience with high save rates and playlist curation behavior generates a streaming growth curve that infrastructure players can model forward and invest against. A majority-male audience generating passive streams generates lower per-listener engagement value and a flatter growth curve.
25M+ monthly listeners · $50M JV · Zero catalog ownership concession
The Formula Distilled

Step 1: Build a loyal core audience (Faiyaz's male underground base, 2016–2020). Step 2: Release a "bridge single" with new emotional access points without abandoning sonic identity ("Wasting Time," 2021). Step 3: Deliver a project-scale statement that fully operationalizes the expanded emotional range ("Wasteland," 2022). Step 4: Watch the algorithmic signals change. Step 5: Infrastructure comes to you.

The key word is authentic. Faiyaz didn't become soft. He revealed more dimension. He didn't chase female listeners with content designed for them. He made music about the emotional experiences women recognize across the table from men — and made it safe to feel those things through his lens.

The question is not "how do I flip my audience?" The question is: which songs in the existing catalog do you sequence, re-promote, or lean into to generate female-coded engagement signals — and how does the new release schedule amplify that?

The Key Insight About Existing Catalog

Most artists think audience growth only comes from new releases. But Spotify's algorithm doesn't care about release date. If an existing track in your catalog suddenly starts getting saved, playlist-added, and shared at high rates — the algorithm will push it to new listeners exactly the same as a new release. Re-activating the right back-catalog tracks with the right framing is a zero-cost growth mechanism. Faiyaz did this with his early Sonder catalog — those songs kept accumulating listens years after release because his new audience sought out everything he'd made.

Part 5

The Content Layer — What Faiyaz Did and What Ethan Payton Can Do Better

Faiyaz's content strategy was built on intentional scarcity. He posted infrequently but with high visual quality and emotional resonance. He didn't explain his music. He let it breathe. He shared fragments — a snippet of a session, a visual that felt like album art, an occasional unfiltered moment.

The result: every post felt like an event. Female fans specifically shared his content because it made them feel like insiders in something rare.

Ethan has something Faiyaz doesn't have: multiple creative disciplines operating simultaneously. Animation, drawing, guitar, music production — all happening in the same brain that makes the records. This is not a marketing advantage. It is a depth of person advantage. When someone sees you drawing at 2am and hears the track playing in the background, they're not seeing a content strategy. They're seeing a human being. That's what women specifically respond to — not performance, but presence.

The Content Framework That Does the Flip

Tier 1: Emotional Access Content (highest female resonance)

  • Guitar riffs that became songs: A 30-second clip of you finding the line that became Sweet Frustration or LID. No talking required. Just the sound and the feeling of discovery. Women save this. They share this.
  • Sketches/animations made while listening to the music: You drawing while the beat plays — or revealing a visual you made that represents the song's feeling. This is content that no other artist in your lane can produce, because they don't have this skill. It creates a completely unique emotional entry point.
  • The moment before the vocal: A clip from the session of you about to record the hook — not the performance itself, but the breath before. The preparation. Women respond to vulnerability in process, not just in lyrics.

Tier 2: Identity Expansion Content (builds three-dimensionality)

  • "I made this while ___": Context for when and why a song was made. "I made Distance when I was 23 and didn't know how to say this out loud." This retroactively layers feeling onto existing catalog tracks and sends female listeners into the back catalog.
  • The non-music you: Your gaming references, your animation work, your actual opinions about things. The lover boy aspect your girlfriend identified doesn't need to be performed — it needs to surface naturally through the regular posts that reveal who you actually are when you're not in artist mode.
  • The health/mind content: The creatine transcript you're reading, the bike rides, the healthy grind aesthetic. Larry June built a massive female audience partly because women found the "healthy, disciplined man who takes care of himself" aesthetic genuinely compelling in an era of chaos. You have the same raw material.

Tier 3: Reactive/Relational Content (lowers the barrier)

  • Responding to comments in the music: Not Q&As. Reading a comment that resonated about one of your songs and letting it land — a pause, a "yeah" — that's it. Women share the artist who shows he actually hears what his music does to people.
  • Duets/stitches with fans using your music: When someone makes a GRWM or night drive Reel with Hollywood Fever or Roll With It, stitch it. This floods their female audience into your profile and creates reciprocal loyalty. Faiyaz was too private to do this. You can be more accessible than him in this one specific area without losing the mystique.

What Success Looks Like — Milestones for the Flip

The gender flip is not a single event. It's a slow rebalancing that compounds. Here's what to watch for, in sequence:

Signal 1 — April to June 2026

Sweet Frustration's save rate is higher than Hollywood Fever's was in its first 60 days. Even if the stream count is lower, the higher save rate means you've reached a new type of listener. Watch your Spotify for Artists "audience" page for gender breakdown movement — even a 2–3% shift toward female signals the strategy is working.

Signal 2 — July to September 2026

You start seeing "late night," "feelings," "emotional R&B," "night drive," and "feminine energy" playlist names in your Spotify for Artists "playlists" section. These are playlist categories dominated by female curators. When your tracks start appearing in them organically — without you submitting — the flip is happening algorithmically.

Signal 3 — September to December 2026

Back catalog tracks (Turn Me Down, Difficult, Ride With Me, Origami) that have been dormant show renewed streaming activity without you releasing anything new. This is the downstream effect of the Wasteland moment — new fans seeking the full catalog. When this starts, the algorithm has fully re-indexed your profile as "artist worth exploring backward."

Signal 4 — 2027

Your gender split is at or approaching 45/55 F. Monthly listeners are above 10K and holding between releases. You now have the audience profile that sync licensing supervisors, playlist editors, and infrastructure partners can model forward. This is when you evaluate CREAM album release, distribution upgrade, and whether any JV conversations are worth having — from strength, not desperation.
